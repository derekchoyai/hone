# Example — Research Review

**Illustrates:** an AI research summary full of confident, specific, *unsourced* claims —
the false-confidence trap. Fluent prose reads as rigor.

---

## The work (AI-generated, pasted by the user)

> **Summary:** The remote-work productivity debate is settled. Studies show remote workers
> are 13% more productive, companies save $11,000 per employee annually, and 77% of remote
> workers report higher job satisfaction. Remote work is unambiguously a net positive.

**Context / stakes:** "Slide in a board deck arguing we go remote-first."

## Classification
- **Domain:** research · **Risk:** high (board decision, org-wide) · **Depth:** 7

## Work map (excerpt)
- **Claims:** +13% productivity; $11k/yr savings; 77% higher satisfaction; "settled / unambiguous."
- **Evidence:** three specific statistics, **no sources cited**.
- **Risks:** stats may be from one non-generalizable study; "settled" overstates a contested field.

## Interview (human's actual answers)

> **Q: What are the sources behind the three statistics, and how solid are they?**
> "The AI included them, so I assume they're from real studies."

> **Q: The 13% figure — do you know the study, the population, and whether it generalizes to your company?**
> "No. It sounds like the Stanford one maybe?"

> **Q: Which of these three numbers did you personally verify?**
> "None yet."

> **Q: Is the productivity research actually 'settled'?**
> "The summary said so."

> **Q: What would a skeptic on your board ask first?**
> "Probably for the sources."

> **Q: What are you least confident about?**
> "Where the numbers came from."

### Your judgment gaps
- The 13% figure most likely traces to a **single call-center study (Bloom et al.)** whose
  setup may not generalize to your company — but you'd be asserting it as universal.
- The field is **actively contested**, not "settled." That single word is your biggest
  exposure in a board room.
- Three precise, citation-free statistics is the classic AI false-confidence pattern.

### Verify before you ship
- [ ] Find and read the primary source for each of the three numbers.
- [ ] Confirm each study's population/setup actually transfers to your context.
- [ ] Replace "settled/unambiguous" with a calibrated claim and the real tradeoffs.

### How to defend this
> "Source for the 13%, and does it apply to us?" — currently **no answer**. That's the gap.

### Practice next
For any AI-provided statistic, locate the primary source *before* it enters a deck. A
number you can't source is a number you can't defend.

---

**The point:** the summary is articulate and might even be roughly right — but presented
with judgment this thin, it's a liability, not evidence.
